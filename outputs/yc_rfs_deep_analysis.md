# YC Requests for Startups - Deep Analysis and Ratings

Date: July 2, 2026

This memo analyzes the YC problem statements from the attached text, covering the Summer 2026 and Spring 2026 RFS lists. I treated each heading as a separate opportunity. "Startups That Want to Sell to Huge Companies" is a GTM thesis rather than a product category, so it is rated as a go-to-market route.

## How to Read the Scores

All scores are 1-10, where 10 is best.

- Impact: potential economic, societal, or strategic impact if the company works.
- Customer ROI: how clearly a buyer gets paid back through lower cost, higher revenue, reduced risk, or faster cycle time.
- Startup ROI: attractiveness for founders/investors after factoring market size, margins, exit potential, and risk.
- Feasibility: likelihood a strong startup team can build a credible wedge in 12-24 months.
- Speed: expected speed to paid pilots or revenue.
- Moat: defensibility if the company gets traction.
- Capital efficiency: ability to get meaningful traction without huge upfront capital.
- Overall: my combined judgment, not a pure average.

## Best Bets by Founder Type

For a software-heavy founding team without deep hardware, biotech, or government background, the strongest starting points are AI-Native Service Companies, Software for Agents, Company Brain or AI OS for Companies, Cursor for Product Managers, SaaS Challengers, and Make LLMs Easy to Train.

For a team with rare domain access, the best asymmetric bets are Infra for Government Fraud Hunters, Supply Chain 2.0 for Semiconductors, Counter-Swarm Defense, AI Guidance for Physical Work, and Low-Pesticide Agriculture.

For a deeptech team with strong technical credibility and capital access, the highest-upside moonshots are Large Spatial Models, Inference Chips for Agent Workflows, AI-Native Discovery Engines, Electronics in Space, Modern Metal Mills, and Industrial Capabilities in Space.

## Deep Analysis by Problem Statement

### 1. AI for Low-Pesticide Agriculture

The core problem is the pesticide treadmill: pests and weeds evolve resistance, farmers spray more, input costs rise, and environmental or health concerns increase. The strongest wedge is not "AI for all farming"; it is a narrow crop, region, and pest where chemical spend and yield loss are both painful. Computer vision spot-spraying, pest scouting, precision application, and biological replacement products are the most plausible entry points.

Impact is extremely high because a successful company could reduce chemical usage, protect yields, improve soil and water outcomes, and lower food production costs. Customer ROI can be strong if the product reduces pesticide spend, labor, crop loss, or regulatory exposure in a single growing season. The challenge is that agricultural proof cycles are seasonal, field conditions are messy, and distribution through farmers, co-ops, OEMs, and agronomists is slow.

Best MVP: retrofit camera and decision system for one high-value crop, proving reduced chemical use per acre without yield loss. Best moat: proprietary field dataset, agronomic validation, equipment integrations, and grower trust. Main risk: building elegant AI that fails under dust, weather, crop variation, and farmer economics.

Rating: Impact 10, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 7, Feasibility 4, Speed 4, Moat 8, Capital Efficiency 3, Overall 7.1.

### 2. AI-Native Discovery Engines

This is about moving from research copilots to closed-loop discovery systems that propose, test, interpret, and improve hypotheses. The best versions will pick one scientific domain where data, lab workflows, and value of discovery line up: drug candidates, protein engineering, battery materials, catalysts, polymers, or semiconductors. Broad "scientist assistant" products will be less defensible than systems tied to real experiments.

Impact is enormous because better discovery engines can compress years of research into months and create new drugs, materials, and industrial processes. Startup ROI can be exceptional if the company owns resulting IP or becomes the system of record for high-value R&D. But near-term ROI is hurt by wet-lab costs, data quality, slow validation, and the gap between benchmark performance and real scientific novelty.

Best MVP: closed-loop optimization for one measurable experimental objective, with a partner lab or internal lab. Best moat: proprietary experimental data generated by the loop, not just model prompts. Main risk: selling AI demos instead of producing validated discoveries.

Rating: Impact 10, Customer ROI 7, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 4, Speed 4, Moat 9, Capital Efficiency 4, Overall 7.3.

### 3. AI-Native Service Companies

This is one of the most practical and highest-ROI categories. The key idea is to sell completed work, not software seats. Many outsourced services already have budgets, repeated workflows, defined deliverables, and quality benchmarks, which makes them easier to replace with an AI-native operation.

The strongest verticals are services with high labor content, repetitive judgment, document workflows, and clear buyer pain: insurance brokerage, claims support, tax prep, bookkeeping, audit evidence collection, compliance monitoring, revenue cycle management, prior authorization, and healthcare administration. Customer ROI is obvious when the buyer can compare the startup's output against headcount, consultants, BPOs, or slow internal teams.

Best MVP: take one narrow service, do it manually plus AI, charge for the outcome, then automate the repeatable core. Best moat: workflow ownership, proprietary operating data, QA systems, compliance, and distribution in a vertical. Main risk: becoming a low-margin agency because the founders never productize the service engine.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 9, Startup ROI 9, Feasibility 8, Speed 8, Moat 6, Capital Efficiency 9, Overall 8.7.

### 4. AI Personalized Medicine

The opportunity is to combine personal health data, diagnostics, genomics, wearables, EHR data, and AI reasoning into more tailored care decisions. The near-term startup wedge should avoid making unsupported clinical claims and instead focus on high-trust, bounded use cases: rare disease navigation, oncology trial matching, genetic report interpretation, medication reconciliation, care planning, or clinician-facing decision support.

Impact is extremely high because personalized detection and treatment could change outcomes for severe disease. Customer ROI can be high for payers, providers, and patients when the product prevents late-stage treatment costs or accelerates the right therapy. The problem is that clinical validation, regulatory compliance, privacy, reimbursement, and liability make this one of the hardest categories.

Best MVP: a clinician-in-the-loop product for one specialty with measurable improvement in diagnostic speed or treatment matching. Best moat: longitudinal patient data, clinical partnerships, regulatory credibility, and reimbursement relationships. Main risk: overpromising "personalized medicine" before safety, evidence, and workflow trust are earned.

Rating: Impact 10, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 3, Speed 3, Moat 9, Capital Efficiency 3, Overall 7.0.

### 5. Company Brain

The problem is not document search. It is operational memory: the exact way a company handles exceptions, incidents, refunds, pricing, onboarding, support escalation, and approvals. As agents become capable of doing work, they need structured, current, executable context.

Customer ROI is strong because the product reduces onboarding time, repeated mistakes, support escalations, tribal knowledge loss, and human bottlenecks. The best wedge is a narrow function where actions are frequent and mistakes are costly, such as customer support policies, sales approval logic, IT runbooks, or engineering incident response. A company-wide brain from day one is too broad.

Best MVP: ingest messy knowledge for one workflow and output agent-usable SOPs with citations, permissions, tests, and update loops. Best moat: integrations, action logs, permission-aware knowledge graphs, and becoming the trusted layer between company data and agents. Main risk: being perceived as another enterprise search chatbot.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 9, Feasibility 7, Speed 7, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 8, Overall 8.2.

### 6. Counter-Swarm Defense

This is a strategic defense opportunity driven by the cost asymmetry between cheap drones and expensive interceptors. The most attractive startup shape is not necessarily a weapons company; it could be a real-time distributed system for sensor fusion, threat classification, command, and response across many defenders.

Impact is very high because drone swarms threaten military bases, airports, energy infrastructure, data centers, and public events. Customer ROI is also high because the buyer is avoiding catastrophic loss. The hard parts are procurement, field testing, export controls, safety, reliability, and proving effectiveness against adaptive adversaries.

Best MVP: software that fuses radar, RF, optical, and acoustic inputs into one operational picture for critical infrastructure or defense customers. Best moat: real-world threat data, certifications, deployment experience, and integration with multiple sensor and effector systems. Main risk: long defense sales cycles and hardware-heavy validation.

Rating: Impact 10, Customer ROI 9, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 4, Speed 5, Moat 8, Capital Efficiency 4, Overall 7.5.

### 7. Dynamic Software Interfaces

This opportunity imagines software that users or their agents can reshape for their own workflows. The deep insight is that AI could make every user their own forward-deployed engineer. The practical wedge is likely internal enterprise software, where customization pain is already expensive and users tolerate workflow-specific interfaces.

Impact is meaningful but uncertain because most users do not want to design software from scratch. Customer ROI is strongest for teams that already pay consultants or internal admins to customize Salesforce, ERP, dashboards, support consoles, or operations tools. The product must balance flexibility with safety, maintainability, permissions, and coherent UX.

Best MVP: an agent-editable UI layer for one internal tool category, with sandboxed changes and admin approval. Best moat: a runtime for safe customization, reusable primitives, and learned patterns across many customers. Main risk: too much freedom creates brittle, inconsistent software.

Rating: Impact 7, Customer ROI 6, Startup ROI 7, Feasibility 6, Speed 5, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 8, Overall 6.6.

### 8. Electronics in Space

The thesis is that cheaper launch and more activity in orbit will create demand for compute in space, especially inference chips optimized for radiation, mass, power, and thermal constraints. The market is real but specialized. Buyers care about reliability and flight heritage more than normal SaaS-style speed.

Impact is high because onboard inference enables autonomous satellites, Earth observation, defense systems, robotics, and deep-space missions. Startup ROI can be strong if the company becomes a standard space compute module provider. The downside is that chip design, radiation hardening, qualification, and customer adoption cycles are slow and capital-intensive.

Best MVP: not a full custom chip immediately, but a space-qualified inference module, compiler, or board-level system using existing silicon where possible. Best moat: flight heritage, radiation test data, thermal/power design, and satellite operator relationships. Main risk: market timing and qualification cycles outlasting startup runway.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 3, Speed 3, Moat 9, Capital Efficiency 2, Overall 6.8.

### 9. Hardware Supply Chain

The problem is iteration speed for hardware teams. China has dense supplier networks and fast design-to-part loops; the US system often has long quoting, manufacturing, and logistics delays. A startup does not need to rebuild all manufacturing to win; it can attack one painful bottleneck.

Customer ROI is strong because every week saved in prototyping can save engineering payroll, accelerate product launch, and increase learning speed. The best wedges are rapid RFQ, manufacturability checks, supplier matching, quality control, and logistics for one part category like CNC, sheet metal, actuators, PCBs, injection molding, or robotics components.

Best MVP: a vertical procurement and iteration platform for a specific hardware community, with guaranteed turnaround and quality. Best moat: supplier network density, pricing data, manufacturability feedback, and repeat orders. Main risk: marketplace complexity and quality failures.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 7, Feasibility 6, Speed 6, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 6, Overall 7.2.

### 10. Industrial Capabilities in Space

This is a true frontier moonshot: extracting and processing raw materials in space or on the moon, then manufacturing structures from local resources. The impact could be civilizational if off-world infrastructure becomes real. However, the near-term business case is much weaker than the long-term story.

Customer ROI is not yet obvious outside government, space agencies, defense, and a small set of lunar infrastructure companies. Startup ROI depends on grants, contracts, technical milestones, and being early to a market that may take a decade or more. The capital requirement is very high.

Best MVP: a component technology for regolith processing, electrolysis, high-temperature operations, or additive manufacturing that can be validated on Earth first. Best moat: patents, mission partnerships, and accumulated test data. Main risk: market arrives later than the company can survive.

Rating: Impact 9, Customer ROI 6, Startup ROI 7, Feasibility 2, Speed 1, Moat 10, Capital Efficiency 1, Overall 5.8.

### 11. Inference Chips for Agent Workflows

The insight is that agent workloads are not simple one-shot inference. They involve loops, tool calls, memory movement, branching, context reuse, speculative execution, and orchestration. The opportunity may be less about one chip and more about the architecture, compiler, runtime, memory hierarchy, and benchmarking stack for agentic workloads.

Impact and startup ROI are huge if a startup can prove a major performance or cost advantage. But feasibility is extremely low for a typical startup because silicon is expensive, incumbents are powerful, and customers demand reliability at scale. The better wedge may be a compiler or runtime that demonstrates agent-workload speedups on existing hardware before raising for silicon.

Best MVP: agent trace benchmarks plus a runtime/compiler layer that improves utilization, context reuse, or routing across models. Best moat: workload data, compiler IP, customer integration, and eventual hardware design. Main risk: trying to outspend NVIDIA, Google, and hyperscalers before proving a focused wedge.

Rating: Impact 9, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 9, Feasibility 2, Speed 2, Moat 10, Capital Efficiency 1, Overall 7.0.

### 12. SaaS Challengers

The broad thesis is that AI lowers software development cost enough to attack legacy SaaS incumbents. This is powerful but dangerous because "clone Salesforce cheaper" is rarely enough. The best opportunities are expensive, hated, implementation-heavy systems with poor UX and high services spend.

Customer ROI can be high if the startup reduces seat cost, services cost, setup time, or workflow complexity. Startup ROI is high because many legacy SaaS markets are massive. The key is to avoid broad horizontal categories with low switching urgency and instead pick a wedge with a clear migration path.

Best MVP: AI-native replacement for one painful module inside ERP, supply chain, compliance, EDA, industrial systems, or vertical back office software. Best moat: migration tooling, workflow data, integrations, and becoming the operating system for a specific role. Main risk: underestimating trust, permissions, edge cases, and incumbent lock-in.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 9, Feasibility 7, Speed 7, Moat 6, Capital Efficiency 9, Overall 8.0.

### 13. Software for Agents

This category is about building software where agents are first-class users. Human-first interfaces are slow and brittle for agents; agent-first products need APIs, MCP servers, CLIs, machine-readable docs, predictable auth, audit logs, and programmatic onboarding.

Impact is high because a large agent ecosystem will need reliable tools to act in the world. Customer ROI is strong for companies whose agents waste time fighting UI or brittle browser automations. Startup ROI is attractive because the category is early, capital efficient, and platform-like.

Best MVP: pick one high-frequency agent task, such as procurement, CRM updates, travel booking, data enrichment, compliance evidence, or payments, and build the agent-native system of record. Best moat: reliability, documentation quality, trust, network effects, and becoming the default tool agents call. Main risk: standards and buyer behavior are still forming.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 9, Feasibility 8, Speed 7, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 9, Overall 8.3.

### 14. Startups That Want to Sell to Huge Companies

This is not a product idea; it is a change in enterprise GTM. The claim is that AI lets tiny teams build enterprise-depth products faster, while large companies are actively looking for AI-native vendors. This creates a real opening for startups with narrow, executive-visible solutions.

Customer ROI can be very high because Fortune 100 buyers have large problems, large budgets, and clear urgency around AI transformation. Startup ROI can also be high because a single enterprise contract can create meaningful revenue early. The danger is pilot purgatory, procurement drag, security reviews, and building bespoke work for one customer.

Best MVP: a narrow workflow with a named executive owner, measurable value, and a deployment path that does not require replacing a core system. Best moat: enterprise references, compliance posture, workflow embed, and expansion across business units. Main risk: confusing a paid pilot with repeatable product-market fit.

Rating: Impact 7, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 6, Speed 5, Moat 5, Capital Efficiency 8, Overall 7.2.

### 15. Supply Chain 2.0 for Semiconductors

Semiconductor supply chains are complex, geopolitical, capacity-constrained, and still managed through old systems. The most urgent wedges are advanced packaging, HBM visibility, wafer allocation, export compliance, supplier risk, and multi-tier dependency mapping. This is a domain where generic supply chain software is too shallow.

Impact is high because bottlenecks in chips can delay AI infrastructure, cars, defense systems, and consumer electronics. Customer ROI is high because a single supply disruption can cost more than the software budget many times over. But the company needs deep semiconductor expertise and access to sensitive supplier data.

Best MVP: a risk and allocation platform for one bottleneck, such as advanced packaging or HBM, sold to chip designers, OEMs, or procurement teams. Best moat: proprietary supplier graph, compliance logic, real-time signals, and deep ERP/SAP integration. Main risk: being locked out of data by incumbents and supplier secrecy.

Rating: Impact 9, Customer ROI 9, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 5, Speed 5, Moat 8, Capital Efficiency 6, Overall 7.6.

### 16. The AI Operating System for Companies

This overlaps with Company Brain but is more ambitious: make the whole company queryable and turn work artifacts into a closed feedback loop. It connects Slack, Linear, GitHub, Notion, calls, tickets, docs, and customer data, then helps the company sense, decide, and act.

Customer ROI is strong when the product reduces wasted engineering work, shortens planning cycles, improves customer feedback loops, and generates specs agents can execute. The best wedge is likely engineering/product/customer feedback, because artifacts are abundant and outcomes are measurable. However, there are privacy, surveillance, and integration risks.

Best MVP: one closed loop from customer signal to product decision to engineering task to outcome measurement. Best moat: company context graph, agent-executable specs, process telemetry, and trust layer. Main risk: becoming a dashboard or meeting summarizer instead of the operating layer for decisions.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 9, Feasibility 7, Speed 7, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 8, Overall 8.3.

### 17. Cursor for Product Managers

This opportunity targets the "what should we build?" loop rather than the coding loop. The product would ingest interviews, support tickets, usage data, sales calls, churn reasons, and competitive context, then produce feature recommendations, PRDs, prototypes, and coding-agent-ready implementation tasks.

Customer ROI is high because building the wrong feature is expensive, and product discovery is often messy. Feasibility is also high because the data sources already exist in tools like Gong, Intercom, Linear, Jira, Amplitude, Notion, and GitHub. The danger is that PM work includes judgment, taste, strategy, politics, and prioritization, which are harder than summarization.

Best MVP: a product discovery cockpit for startups or growth teams that turns real customer evidence into a scoped feature proposal and dev-ready task breakdown. Best moat: feedback corpus, integrations, evaluation loops that compare recommendations to outcomes, and tight connection to coding agents. Main risk: crowded AI note-taking/product analytics market.

Rating: Impact 7, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 8, Speed 7, Moat 6, Capital Efficiency 9, Overall 8.0.

### 18. AI-Native Hedge Funds

This is a high-upside but brutal category. AI agents can read filings, earnings calls, transcripts, news, alternative data, and code research workflows faster than humans. But markets are adversarial, alpha decays, and every serious fund is trying some version of this.

Customer ROI is unusual because the "customer" is the fund's own capital or LP capital. If it works, ROI is massive; if it does not, there is no forgiving enterprise buyer. Feasibility is low because of compliance, data cost, backtesting traps, model drift, market impact, and the difficulty of proving repeatable edge.

Best MVP: either a narrow strategy with live paper-trading proof or AI research infrastructure sold to existing funds before launching a fund. Best moat: proprietary data, execution, risk systems, and a repeatable research factory. Main risk: confusing impressive agent research with durable alpha.

Rating: Impact 5, Customer ROI 9, Startup ROI 7, Feasibility 4, Speed 3, Moat 8, Capital Efficiency 5, Overall 6.3.

### 19. AI-Native Agencies

This is one of the fastest paths to revenue. Agencies, design firms, ad shops, legal document firms, content studios, recruiting firms, and implementation shops can use AI to produce more output per person and sell outcomes rather than tools. It is founder-friendly because customers already understand the buying motion.

Customer ROI is strong when the agency delivers faster or cheaper work at equal quality. Startup ROI is good but not automatically venture-scale; the founder must convert services into a repeatable software-driven operating system. Otherwise the company may be profitable but valued like an agency.

Best MVP: pick one deliverable with clear before/after value, such as ad creatives, landing pages, sales collateral, contract review, compliance packets, or video production. Best moat: distribution, brand, proprietary workflow system, and data from repeated engagements. Main risk: commoditization and low defensibility.

Rating: Impact 6, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 7, Feasibility 9, Speed 9, Moat 4, Capital Efficiency 10, Overall 7.8.

### 20. Stablecoin Financial Services

Stablecoin infrastructure is moving from crypto-native speculation toward regulated payments, settlement, treasury, and cross-border money movement. The opportunity is not just issuing stablecoins; it is building useful financial services around them under compliance constraints.

Customer ROI is strongest for cross-border businesses, exporters, global payroll, marketplaces, remittances, treasury management, and high-friction settlement workflows. Startup ROI is high if the company secures trust, licenses, banking relationships, and distribution. The risks are regulation, custody, fraud, AML/KYC, liquidity, and consumer protection.

Best MVP: a stablecoin treasury or payment product for one business segment where speed, FX, and banking friction are painful. Best moat: compliance stack, banking partners, liquidity, licenses, risk controls, and trust. Main risk: building generic crypto finance without a specific regulated use case.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 5, Speed 5, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 6, Overall 7.4.

### 21. AI for Government

Government agencies face rising inbound paperwork, benefits applications, permits, case processing, and citizen service demands. AI can reduce backlogs and improve responsiveness if it is deployed safely. The opportunity is real, but procurement is hard.

Impact is high because better government operations affect millions of people. Customer ROI can be high because agencies spend heavily on labor, contractors, legacy systems, and backlogs. The best wedge is a low-risk workflow where AI assists humans and improves throughput without making final sensitive decisions alone.

Best MVP: intake triage, document extraction, case summarization, permit routing, call center support, or benefits workflow assistance for one agency type. Best moat: procurement experience, security certifications, references, and integration with legacy systems. Main risk: slow sales cycles and political resistance.

Rating: Impact 9, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 7, Feasibility 5, Speed 3, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 7, Overall 6.9.

### 22. Modern Metal Mills

The opportunity is to rebuild or modernize metal production around speed, flexibility, automation, and energy optimization. The customer pain is clear: long lead times, fragmented systems, thin margins, and aging operations. The market is strategically important for reindustrialization.

Customer ROI is very high if a startup can compress lead times from weeks to days or materially lower energy and setup costs. But building a new mill is capital-intensive and operationally difficult. A more feasible startup wedge may be software and automation for existing mills: quoting, scheduling, MES, inspection, changeovers, or energy management.

Best MVP: AI planning and quoting layer for a specific mill type, such as aluminum rolling or steel tube, proving better utilization and faster short runs. Best moat: operational data, automation integration, energy contracts, and eventually owned physical capacity. Main risk: capex and industrial execution complexity.

Rating: Impact 9, Customer ROI 9, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 3, Speed 3, Moat 9, Capital Efficiency 2, Overall 6.9.

### 23. AI Guidance for Physical Work

This is one of the strongest practical opportunities because it applies AI to labor shortages in the physical world. Instead of replacing workers, AI sees what they see and guides them through tasks. The best wedges are jobs with repeatable procedures, high training cost, high error cost, and enough visual context.

Customer ROI is strong in HVAC, manufacturing maintenance, telecom field service, logistics, nursing support, aviation maintenance, oil and gas, and industrial inspection. Reducing training time, rework, downtime, and safety incidents creates a clear economic case. Feasibility is good because phones, headsets, cameras, and smart glasses already exist, but reliability and liability are serious.

Best MVP: one vertical workflow with step-by-step visual guidance, escalation to experts, and automatic documentation. Best moat: procedural video dataset, integrations with work-order systems, certifications, and workflow-specific QA. Main risk: real-world failure modes where model confidence exceeds actual safety.

Rating: Impact 9, Customer ROI 9, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 7, Speed 6, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 7, Overall 8.1.

### 24. Large Spatial Models

This is a foundation-model-scale bet for spatial reasoning, geometry, manipulation, CAD, robotics, construction, simulation, and physical design. If it works, it could unlock a major new layer of AI capability. But general spatial intelligence is a very expensive research problem.

Impact is extremely high because spatial reasoning is essential for robotics, manufacturing, architecture, games, simulation, AR, logistics, and embodied AI. Startup ROI could be huge, but feasibility for a small team is low unless they choose a narrow domain first. The near-term wedge should be a spatial model for one valuable workflow rather than "OpenAI for 3D" from day one.

Best MVP: domain-specific spatial reasoning for warehouse robotics, CAD automation, construction plans, manufacturing inspection, or robot manipulation. Best moat: proprietary 3D/spatial datasets, evals, simulation loops, and deployment integrations. Main risk: requiring too much compute and research before revenue.

Rating: Impact 10, Customer ROI 7, Startup ROI 9, Feasibility 2, Speed 1, Moat 10, Capital Efficiency 1, Overall 6.7.

### 25. Infra for Government Fraud Hunters

This is a very interesting high-ROI niche. The buyer is not generic government; it is whistleblower law firms, state AGs, inspectors general, auditors, and investigators who need to turn messy tips and documents into evidence-backed cases. The False Claims Act and related recovery mechanisms create a direct economic incentive.

Impact is high because successful tools could return money to taxpayers and deter fraud. Customer ROI is excellent: a tool that speeds case building, entity tracing, document review, or complaint preparation can pay for itself through larger and faster recoveries. Founder-market fit matters more here than in most categories.

Best MVP: secure evidence assembly for one case type, with PDF ingestion, entity graphs, timeline construction, claims matching, and exportable legal packets. Best moat: domain-specific workflows, legal trust, case corpus, citation quality, and relationships with investigators. Main risk: legal accuracy, sensitive data, and long case timelines.

Rating: Impact 9, Customer ROI 10, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 6, Speed 5, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 8, Overall 7.8.

### 26. Make LLMs Easy to Train

Training and post-training models is still painfully operational. Teams struggle with data management, GPU reliability, experiment tracking, broken open-source tooling, cloud setup, evals, debugging, and cost control. As model specialization grows, this tooling becomes more important.

Customer ROI is high because GPU waste and researcher time are expensive. Startup ROI is strong if the company becomes core infrastructure for model labs, enterprises, and AI-native software teams. The category is competitive, so the wedge must be sharp rather than "better ML platform."

Best MVP: one painful part of the training loop, such as reliable training jobs, dataset versioning at terabyte scale, eval-driven post-training, debugging failed GPU runs, or a research-native dev environment. Best moat: reliability, workflow depth, data layer, integrations across clouds, and accumulated failure knowledge. Main risk: hyperscalers, open-source tools, and fast-changing model workflows.

Rating: Impact 8, Customer ROI 8, Startup ROI 8, Feasibility 7, Speed 6, Moat 7, Capital Efficiency 8, Overall 7.9.

## Highest-ROI Startup Ideas to Actually Start

If I had to choose the most attractive starting points for a small team, I would pick:

1. AI-native service company in compliance, healthcare admin, insurance ops, or accounting.
2. Agent-first software layer for one workflow agents already struggle with.
3. Company Brain for one narrow function, especially support, sales approvals, or incident response.
4. AI guidance for one skilled physical-work vertical like HVAC, manufacturing maintenance, or telecom field service.
5. Cursor for PMs with a tight customer-feedback-to-dev-task loop.
6. LLM training/post-training tooling focused on one painful failure mode.
7. Government fraud investigation infrastructure, but only with a domain-expert founder.
8. Semiconductor supply chain visibility, but only with deep semiconductor access.

## Ideas I Would Avoid Unless the Team Has Rare Fit

- Industrial Capabilities in Space: too long-horizon and capital-intensive for most founders.
- Large Spatial Models: excellent mission, but needs research depth, compute, and a narrow wedge.
- Inference Chips for Agent Workflows: massive upside, but full-stack silicon is brutal without elite chip and compiler credibility.
- AI Personalized Medicine: huge impact, but medical evidence, regulation, and liability are non-negotiable.
- Modern Metal Mills: compelling, but better as software-first mill modernization unless the team can raise and operate serious industrial capacity.
- AI-Native Hedge Funds: attractive upside, but extremely hard to distinguish real alpha from agent-generated noise.

## Bottom Line

The best near-term startup ROI is not in the biggest-sounding moonshots. It is in categories where AI turns expensive human labor into repeatable outcomes with clear budgets: AI-native services, agent-first software, company knowledge/action layers, physical-work guidance, and training infrastructure. The deeptech categories may produce larger companies, but only for teams with rare technical credibility, domain access, and patient capital.
